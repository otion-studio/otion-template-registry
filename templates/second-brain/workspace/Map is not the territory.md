# Map is not the territory

A map is a useful, simplified model of reality — but it is not reality. Confusing the two is one of the most common and expensive thinking errors. The phrase comes from Alfred Korzybski: any description, theory, or plan leaves things out, and the things it leaves out can be exactly the ones that bite you.

Every model you use — a financial forecast, a personality type, a job title, an org chart — is a map. Maps are necessary; you can't hold the whole territory in your head. The danger is forgetting the abstraction is lossy and treating the simplified version as the complete truth. When the map and the territory disagree, the territory wins.

The practical discipline is humility about your own models: hold them loosely, update them when reality pushes back, and go look at the territory directly when the stakes are high. This is why [First principles](First principles.md) and a [Beginner's mind](Beginner's mind.md) matter — they send you back to the ground.
