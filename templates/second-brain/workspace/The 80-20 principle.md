# The 80/20 principle

A small share of causes produces most of the effects. The economist Vilfredo Pareto noticed that 80% of Italy's land was owned by 20% of the people, and the same lopsided pattern shows up almost everywhere: a fifth of your customers drive most of your revenue, a fifth of your tasks deliver most of your results, a handful of notes carry most of your second brain's value.

The exact numbers don't matter — it's rarely exactly 80/20. What matters is the *shape*: contributions are radically unequal, not evenly spread. This contradicts the instinct to treat all tasks, all hours, and all inputs as roughly equal. They aren't. A few are doing the heavy lifting, and most are noise.

The practical move is identification followed by concentration: find the vital few and pour your effort there, then ruthlessly cut, automate, or ignore the trivial many — pure [Via negativa](Via negativa.md). This is the analytical engine behind [The Eisenhower matrix](The Eisenhower matrix.md) and the reason [Deep work](Deep work.md) on the right task beats busy work on ten wrong ones.
