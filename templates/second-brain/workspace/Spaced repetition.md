# Spaced repetition

We forget on a predictable curve — Hermann Ebbinghaus mapped it in the 1880s — and the cure is to review *just* as something is about to slip away. Spaced repetition schedules reviews at growing intervals: a day, then three, then a week, then a month. Each successful recall flattens the forgetting curve and pushes the next review further out.

The mechanism is the *spacing effect*: information reviewed across spread-out sessions sticks far better than the same time spent cramming. Cramming feels productive because everything is fresh, but that fluency is an illusion that evaporates within days. Spacing feels harder — you've half-forgotten by review time — and that effortful retrieval is exactly what builds durable memory.

The pairing matters: spaced repetition works best with *active recall* (trying to retrieve the answer before checking), which is what [The Feynman technique](The Feynman technique.md) forces. Over months, the gains stack — a clean example of [Compounding](Compounding.md) applied to memory.
