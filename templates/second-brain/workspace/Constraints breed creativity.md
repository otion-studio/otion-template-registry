# Constraints breed creativity

A blank page is paralysing; a tight brief is liberating. Limits force you to make choices, and choices are where creativity lives. Give someone infinite options and they freeze; give them three colours, fourteen lines, or a single weekend, and they invent.

The evidence is everywhere in art. The sonnet's fourteen lines and rigid rhyme scheme didn't shackle poets — they gave them a problem interesting enough to solve. Dr. Seuss wrote *Green Eggs and Ham* on a bet that he couldn't do it in fifty distinct words; the constraint produced one of the best-selling children's books ever written. The limit becomes the engine.

The mechanism is focus: constraints shrink the search space so your attention has somewhere to go, and they create productive friction that forces non-obvious solutions. This is the creative face of [Via negativa](Via negativa.md) — removing options to find the good one — and it's why [First principles](First principles.md) thinking, which strips a problem to its irreducible limits, so often unlocks something new.
