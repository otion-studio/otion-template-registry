# The Feynman technique

If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it well enough. The Feynman technique — named for the physicist Richard Feynman, a famously clear teacher — turns that observation into a method for finding the holes in your own knowledge.

It has four steps. Pick a concept and write an explanation as if teaching it to a curious twelve-year-old, in plain words with no jargon. When you stumble or reach for a fancy term to paper over a gap, you've found exactly what you don't understand — go back to the source and relearn that piece. Then simplify again, using analogies, until the explanation flows without effort.

The power is in the *forced simplicity*. Jargon lets you hide confusion even from yourself; plain language won't allow it. The technique is really active recall plus a brutal honesty filter, which is why it pairs so well with [Spaced repetition](Spaced repetition.md) and is the fastest way to test the edge of your [Circle of competence](Circle of competence.md).
