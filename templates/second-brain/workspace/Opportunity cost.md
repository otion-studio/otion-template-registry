# Opportunity cost

The true cost of anything is what you give up to get it. When you say yes to one thing, you're saying no to everything else you could have done with that time, money, or attention. The dollar in your pocket isn't the cost of the coffee — the cost is the *next best use* of that dollar.

Opportunity cost is invisible, which is what makes it dangerous. The thing you chose is right in front of you; the thing you forfeited never happens, so it leaves no trace. A meeting that "only takes an hour" actually costs you the most valuable hour you would otherwise have spent. This is why busy people who say yes to everything often accomplish the least.

The practical move is to make the comparison explicit: not "is this good?" but "is this better than the best alternative?" Almost everything is good enough to justify itself in isolation. The bar that matters is relative. This is the quiet logic behind [Via negativa](Via negativa.md) and the focus demanded by [Deep work](Deep work.md).
