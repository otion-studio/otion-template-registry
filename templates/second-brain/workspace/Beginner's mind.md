# Beginner's mind

"In the beginner's mind there are many possibilities, but in the expert's mind there are few." Shunryu Suzuki's line names a real cost of expertise: the more you know, the more your knowledge filters what you're willing to notice. Beginner's mind is the deliberate practice of dropping that filter and looking at the familiar as if for the first time.

Experts are efficient because they pattern-match — they slot new situations into known categories instantly. That speed is usually a gift, but it quietly blinds you to anything that doesn't fit the pattern. The beginner, who has no patterns, asks the "naive" questions that experts have trained themselves not to ask — and those questions are often where the real insight hides.

The skill is holding both: the expert's competence and the beginner's openness. Keep your hard-won models, but treat them as provisional — remember the [Map is not the territory](Map is not the territory.md). When you feel certain, that's the moment to ask the beginner's question on purpose.
