# Default to action

When the cost of being wrong is low and reversible, bias toward doing rather than deliberating. Most decisions are *two-way doors* — you can walk back through them — and for those, the time spent agonising costs more than the occasional mistake. Action also generates information that no amount of thinking can: you learn what actually happens only by trying.

This isn't recklessness. The discipline is to *distinguish* reversible decisions from irreversible ones. For the rare one-way doors — quitting a job, publishing something permanent, a large irreversible spend — slow down, invert, and reason carefully. For everything else, the longer you wait, the more [Opportunity cost](Opportunity cost.md) you pay for a choice you could have made and corrected in a fraction of the time.

A useful heuristic: if you'd need to think for more than a few minutes about something you can undo, just try it. Action breaks the analysis paralysis that disguises itself as diligence. At the smallest scale, this is [The two-minute rule](The two-minute rule.md); at the largest, it's how creative work actually gets made — see [Idea emergence](Idea emergence.md).
