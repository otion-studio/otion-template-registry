# Second-order thinking

First-order thinking stops at the immediate result: *and then what?* Second-order thinking keeps asking. Every action has consequences, and those consequences have consequences. The people who get blindsided are usually the ones who stopped at step one.

A classic example: a city introduces a bounty for dead rats to fight an infestation. First order — fewer rats. Second order — people start *breeding* rats to collect the bounty. The incentive worked exactly as designed and produced the opposite of what was wanted. Almost every policy, product decision, or personal choice hides a chain like this.

The practical habit is small: when you reach a conclusion, ask "and then what happens?" two or three more times. Most people won't, which is precisely why the discipline pays. It pairs naturally with [Inversion](Inversion.md) — trace the chain forward, then ask what would make it go wrong.
