# Idea emergence

Ideas rarely arrive whole. They surface slowly, often after a long period where nothing seems to be happening — what Steven Johnson calls the "slow hunch." A hunch sits half-formed in the back of your mind for months or years, waiting to collide with another half-formed hunch. The flash of insight in the shower is real, but it's the *last* step of a long, mostly invisible process.

This reframes where ideas come from. The breakthrough isn't a single brilliant moment; it's the moment two slow hunches finally connect. Which means the most useful thing you can do is keep your hunches alive and within reach of each other — write them down, revisit them, and let them sit next to unrelated material until something sparks.

This is the entire argument for a second brain. [Atomic notes](Atomic notes.md) capture hunches before they evaporate, and the dense web of links keeps them bumping into each other. New ideas emerge in the *connections*, not the notes themselves — which is why linking is the real work, and why [The adjacent possible](The adjacent possible.md) describes the space those connections open up.
