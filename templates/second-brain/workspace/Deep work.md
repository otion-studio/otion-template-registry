# Deep work

Deep work is the ability to focus without distraction on a cognitively demanding task. Cal Newport coined the term to name the kind of effort that actually creates value and is genuinely hard to replicate — as opposed to *shallow work*, the email-and-meetings logistics that fills most days but moves little.

The case for it rests on two facts. First, deep work is increasingly rare, because constant connectivity has trained us to flinch toward our phones the moment a task gets hard. Second, it's increasingly valuable, because the problems worth solving are the ones that require sustained concentration. Scarcity plus value is a definition of leverage.

Protecting it is mostly about defense: block the time, kill the notifications, and accept boredom rather than reaching for a quick hit of novelty. The reward is twofold — better output, and the absorbed state described in [Flow](Flow.md). It also depends on choosing the right target first, which is the job of [The Eisenhower matrix](The Eisenhower matrix.md) and [The 80/20 principle](The 80-20 principle.md).
