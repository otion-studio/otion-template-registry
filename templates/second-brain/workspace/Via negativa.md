# Via negativa

Via negativa is improvement by subtraction — getting better by removing what's wrong rather than adding what's new. The term comes from theology (describing God by what he is *not*), but Nassim Taleb popularised it as a way to act under uncertainty: we know what's harmful far more reliably than we know what helps.

Removal is robust in a way that addition isn't. Adding a new tactic, tool, or rule can backfire in ways you can't foresee; taking away a known problem rarely does. A doctor who removes a harmful habit helps the patient more dependably than one who prescribes a new drug with unknown side effects. The same is true of schedules, codebases, and to-do lists: the highest-leverage move is often deletion.

It pairs tightly with [Inversion](Inversion.md) — instead of asking what to add to succeed, ask what to remove to stop failing. In a second brain, via negativa means pruning notes that no longer earn their place, so the web stays alive instead of bloated.
