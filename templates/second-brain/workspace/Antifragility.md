# Antifragility

Some things break under stress (fragile), some resist it (robust), and a rare few actually *improve* from it (antifragile). Nassim Taleb coined the word because the language had no term for the opposite of fragile — and "robust" isn't it. Robust survives the shock unchanged; antifragile uses the shock to grow stronger.

Muscles are antifragile: stress them and they rebuild bigger. So are immune systems, good businesses, and skills practised through difficulty. The key ingredient is *recovery plus adaptation* — small, survivable stressors that trigger improvement, as long as no single shock is large enough to kill you. Remove all stress and you get atrophy, not safety.

The design principle: seek small, frequent failures over rare catastrophic ones, and build slack so you can absorb the inevitable surprise. This is why [Deliberate practice](Deliberate practice.md) deliberately courts difficulty, and why [Via negativa](Via negativa.md) — removing fragilities — often beats adding defenses.
