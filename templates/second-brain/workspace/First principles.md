# First principles

Reasoning from first principles means boiling a problem down to the things you know are true, then building back up from there — instead of reasoning by analogy ("we do it this way because that's how it's done"). Analogy is fast and usually fine; first-principles thinking is slow and occasionally revolutionary.

Aristotle called a first principle "the first basis from which a thing is known." The modern habit is the same: strip away assumptions until you hit bedrock. When the cost of rockets seemed fixed, the first-principles question wasn't "what do rockets cost?" but "what are rockets made of, and what do those raw materials cost?" The gap between the two is where the opportunity lived.

It's effortful, so reserve it for decisions that matter. For everything else, analogy and good [Mental Models](Mental Models.md) are enough. But when you're stuck, or when everyone agrees a little too easily, dropping to first principles is how you find the assumption everyone forgot to question.
