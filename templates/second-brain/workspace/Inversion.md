# Inversion

Instead of asking how to succeed, ask how you would guarantee failure — then avoid that. Inversion turns a problem around and attacks it from the back. It's often easier to see what to *stop* doing than to know what to start.

The mathematician Carl Jacobi was fond of the maxim "invert, always invert," and Charlie Munger built much of his thinking on it: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die, so I'll never go there." If you want a happy team, list everything that would make people quit, and eliminate it. If you want a clear essay, list everything that makes writing unreadable, and cut it.

Inversion is powerful because avoiding stupidity is more reliable than seeking brilliance. The failure modes are usually fewer and more obvious than the paths to success. This is the same instinct behind [Via negativa](Via negativa.md): improve by subtraction.
