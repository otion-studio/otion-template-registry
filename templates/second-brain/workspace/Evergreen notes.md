# Evergreen notes

Most notes are disposable — a meeting scribble, a to-do, a quote you'll never reopen. Evergreen notes are the opposite: written to be developed, revised, and reused across time and projects. Andy Matuschak gave them their name and four principles. They should be *atomic* (one idea each), *concept-oriented* (organised by idea, not by source or date), *densely linked*, and written in your own words as claims you actually believe.

The shift is from collecting to *developing*. A read-it-later pile grows forever and helps no one; an evergreen note gets a little better every time you return to it. You're not archiving information, you're slowly building your own understanding — and because the notes are concept-oriented rather than tied to where you found them, they keep paying off long after the original source is forgotten.

"In your own words" is the load-bearing rule. Copy-pasting a quote leaves the idea outside your head; restating it forces the same understanding check as [The Feynman technique](The Feynman technique.md). Evergreen notes are simply [Atomic notes](Atomic notes.md) tended over time — and that tending is what turns a folder of clippings into a thinking partner.
