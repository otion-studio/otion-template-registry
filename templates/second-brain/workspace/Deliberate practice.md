# Deliberate practice

Time spent is not the same as practice. Most people plateau at "good enough" and then repeat what they can already do for years without improving. Deliberate practice — the concept Anders Ericsson built his research on — is the deliberate opposite: working at the edge of your ability, on the specific things you can't yet do, with immediate feedback.

It has a recognisable shape. You isolate a weakness, design a focused drill that targets it, attempt it, get feedback fast, and adjust. It's effortful and rarely fun, because by definition you spend most of your time slightly failing. That discomfort is the signal that you're in the zone where growth happens — comfort means you've drifted back inside what you already know.

This is why feedback loops are everything: without them you can't tell whether you're improving or just repeating mistakes. It's also why difficulty is a feature, not a bug — the same logic as [Antifragility](Antifragility.md). Practising at the edge mirrors [Beginner's mind](Beginner's mind.md): you stay a student of the parts you haven't mastered.
