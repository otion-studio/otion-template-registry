## Close reading — the method

Close reading means building meaning from the words on the page, not from a summary:

- **Diction** — why *these* words? Note connotations and word choice.
- **Imagery** — what senses does the passage invoke, and to what effect?
- **Form** — meter, rhyme, line breaks, and how they shape meaning.
- **Tone and voice** — who is speaking, and what's their attitude?
- **Tension** — find the contradiction or ambiguity; that's usually the heart of an argument.

## Romantic poetry — what to know

- A reaction against industrial, rational, urban life (ties directly to your History course).
- **Themes:** nature as a moral force, the power of imagination, the individual and emotion, the sublime.
- **Form:** ranges from strict sonnets to loose, conversational blank verse.
- **Key moves:** the speaker often turns from an external scene to an internal reflection.

## Building an essay

- **Thesis** — one arguable sentence stating your claim.
- **Evidence** — short, well-chosen quotations, never long blocks.
- **Analysis** — explain *how* the quotation supports the claim. This is where points live.
- **Structure** — each paragraph advances one sub-claim toward the thesis.

## Key terms

- **Imagery** — language that appeals to the senses.
- **Metaphor / simile** — comparison without / with "like" or "as."
- **Enjambment** — a sentence running past the end of a line.
- **The sublime** — awe mixed with a sense of overwhelming scale or power.
- **Volta** — the "turn" in a poem, often where the argument shifts.
