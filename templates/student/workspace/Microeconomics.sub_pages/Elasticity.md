# Elasticity

Elasticity measures *how much* quantity responds to a change in price. It's the bridge from "which direction" to "by how much."

## Price elasticity of demand

The percentage change in quantity demanded over the percentage change in price:

<!-- otion:math {"equation":"E_{d} = \\frac{\\% \\Delta Q_{d}}{\\% \\Delta P}"} -->

Because the curve slopes down, `Ed` is negative; by convention we talk about its absolute value.

- `|Ed| > 1` → **elastic** (quantity is very responsive; think luxuries).
- `|Ed| < 1` → **inelastic** (quantity barely moves; think gasoline, insulin).
- `|Ed| = 1` → **unit elastic**.

## Why it matters: total revenue

Total revenue is price times quantity. Elasticity tells a seller which way revenue moves when they change price:

<!-- otion:math {"equation":"TR = P \\times Q"} -->

- If demand is **elastic**, raising price *lowers* revenue (you lose more buyers than you gain per unit).
- If demand is **inelastic**, raising price *raises* revenue.

### Worked example

Price rises 10% and quantity demanded falls 4%. Then:

<!-- otion:math {"equation":"E_{d} = \\frac{-4\\%}{+10\\%} = -0.4 \\;\\Rightarrow\\; |E_{d}| = 0.4 < 1"} -->

Demand is **inelastic**, so this price increase *raises* total revenue.

## Determinants of elasticity

- **Substitutes** — more substitutes → more elastic.
- **Necessity vs. luxury** — necessities are inelastic.
- **Time horizon** — demand is more elastic over the long run.
- **Share of budget** — big-ticket items are more elastic.

## Key terms

- **Cross-price elasticity** — response of one good's demand to another's price (positive for substitutes, negative for complements).
- **Income elasticity** — response of demand to income (negative for inferior goods).
- **Price elasticity of supply** — same idea on the seller side.
