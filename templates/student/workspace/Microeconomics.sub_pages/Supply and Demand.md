# Supply and Demand

The model that explains how prices form in a competitive market. Everything else in the course builds on this.

## The two curves

- **Demand** slopes downward: as price falls, buyers want more. Driven by willingness to pay.
- **Supply** slopes upward: as price rises, sellers offer more. Driven by cost of production.

## Equilibrium

The market clears where the curves cross — quantity demanded equals quantity supplied:

<!-- otion:math {"equation":"Q_{d}(P^{*}) = Q_{s}(P^{*})"} -->

At the equilibrium price `P*` there's no shortage and no surplus, so price stops moving.

### Worked example

Suppose demand is `Qd = 100 - 2P` and supply is `Qs = 20 + 2P`. Set them equal:

<!-- otion:math {"equation":"100 - 2P = 20 + 2P \\;\\Rightarrow\\; 80 = 4P \\;\\Rightarrow\\; P^{*} = 20, \\; Q^{*} = 60"} -->

## Shifts vs. movements (the classic trap)

- A **movement along** a curve is caused by a change in the good's **own price**.
- A **shift of** a curve is caused by something else (income, tastes, input costs, number of buyers).

So when a shock hits, ask which one it is, then trace the new equilibrium:

- Income rises (normal good) → **demand shifts right** → higher `P*` and `Q*`.
- Input cost rises → **supply shifts left** → higher `P*`, lower `Q*`.
- New sellers enter → **supply shifts right** → lower `P*`, higher `Q*`.

## Key terms

- **Surplus** — quantity supplied exceeds demanded; price falls.
- **Shortage** — quantity demanded exceeds supplied; price rises.
- **Consumer surplus** — area under demand, above price.
- **Producer surplus** — area above supply, below price.
