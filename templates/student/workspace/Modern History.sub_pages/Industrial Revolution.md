# Industrial Revolution

The shift from hand production to machines and factories, beginning in Britain around 1760. It changed not just how things were made, but where and how people lived.

## Why Britain first?

- **Coal and iron** in abundance, close together.
- **Capital** from colonial trade available to invest.
- **Stable government** and property rights that rewarded innovation.
- **Agricultural surplus** freed labor to move into factories.

## Timeline of key inventions

- **1712** — Newcomen's atmospheric steam engine pumps water from mines.
- **1764** — Hargreaves' spinning jenny multiplies thread output.
- **1769** — Watt's improved steam engine; the era's defining machine.
- **1779** — Crompton's spinning mule combines earlier advances.
- **1825** — Stockton & Darlington, the first public steam railway.
- **1844** — Telegraph lines begin to link cities.

## Social consequences

- **Urbanization** — populations crowded into Manchester, Leeds, Birmingham.
- **Factory system** — long hours, child labor, dangerous conditions.
- **New classes** — an industrial bourgeoisie above a wage-earning proletariat.
- **Reform** — the Factory Acts (from 1833) began limiting hours and child labor.

## Key terms

- **Enclosure** — consolidation of common land into private farms, pushing rural workers toward cities.
- **Laissez-faire** — the doctrine of minimal government interference in the economy.
- **Luddites** — workers who smashed machines in protest (1811–1816).
- **Urbanization** — the growth of the share of people living in cities.
